## `/` — Home

**Purpose:** Get the right person to read the second page. Filter out the wrong person quickly and kindly.

**Above the fold:**
- Wordmark + nav.
- Hero illustration: bristlecone silhouette, weathered, on a ridgeline. Right side.
- Headline (display serif, ~64px): *"Step slowly. Compound for decades."*
- Subhead (~22px, ink-soft): *"Bristlecone is a marketing OS for businesses built to last. It runs your campaigns through a Goal → Strategy → Tactic pipeline with you in command of every consequential decision. It is patient on purpose."*
- Two CTAs: `Join the waitlist` (amber pill) · `Read the philosophy` (ghost link)
- Below CTAs, small italic line: *"We are new. We are small. We are not in a hurry. Neither, we suspect, are you."*

**Sections (in order):**

1. **The honest pitch** — three-column grid, each with a small icon (growth ring, root system, bristlecone needle):
   - *Goal → Strategy → Tactic.* Every campaign decomposes the same way. Nothing happens without a goal that ties to a business outcome.
   - *Operator-in-the-loop.* The system proposes. You commit, revise, or send back to its jurisdiction. It does not act without you.
   - *Evidence over claims.* Every output is traceable to its inputs. No black-box "AI did it." You can read the whole pipeline.

2. **What this is not** — single column, paper-card treatment:
   - Not a growth-hacking tool.
   - Not an AI content firehose.
   - Not a way to "do marketing while you sleep."
   - Not for businesses that need to triple revenue in six months.
   - *(closing line, italic)* If any of those are what you're looking for, we genuinely wish you well — there are better tools out there for that job.

3. **What it actually does** — two-column with a small annotated diagram:
   - Left: short prose, 4 short paragraphs. *"You give it your roots — your offer, your proof, your brand. It plans a season. It drafts the cones — the campaigns — and shows them to you before anything ships. You commit, revise, or push back. When you commit, it executes through real channels. Every decision is recorded in your rings."*
   - Right: simple diagram of the pipeline. Static SVG.

4. **Who it's for** — two side-by-side cards:
   - *Built for:* Businesses with a 10+ year horizon. Solo operators, small teams. People who've been around long enough to be skeptical of marketing tools. Trades, professional services, craft businesses, long-tail SaaS.
   - *Not built for:* Funded startups optimizing for hockey-stick growth. Anyone who needs results next week. Marketers who want full automation with no oversight.

5. **A small proof of craft** — pull-quote treatment, italic display serif:
   > *"The system will not send an email without you approving it. That sounds slow. It is. So is a bristlecone pine."*

6. **CTA band** — soft parchment, centered:
   - *"We open seats slowly, by design."*
   - `Join the waitlist` button + small explainer: *"You'll hear from us when we're ready, not before."*

7. **Footer.**

## `/philosophy`

**Purpose:** The reader is now interested. Earn their trust by showing the belief stack the product is built on.

**Structure:** Long-form essay. Subheaders. No bullet points until the very end. ~1,200–1,800 words.

**Beats to cover:**
- Why "growth" is the wrong frame for most businesses.
- The compounding math of slow, steady customer acquisition vs. boom-bust.
- Why the operator must stay in the loop — not because AI is dumb, but because *judgment* about your business is what you bring.
- What "honest resolution" means: if the system can't actually do something, it says so. It doesn't fall back to a worse option silently.
- The bristlecone pine as a real biological model — not a metaphor we invented for marketing copy. Cite the actual oldest specimens.
- Closing: this is not for everyone. That's a feature.

End with a **belief stack** — six short numbered statements, italicized, no commentary:

1. *Most businesses don't need to grow fast. They need to grow well.*
2. *The operator's judgment is the asset. Software should sharpen it, not replace it.*
3. *A pipeline you can read end-to-end is more trustworthy than a model you can't.*
4. *If the system can't actually do something, it should say so — not fall back to something worse.*
5. *Compounding is boring. That is why it works.*
6. *Patience is a competitive advantage in an impatient market.*

## `/how-it-works`

**Purpose:** Show the actual mechanics without dropping into engineering jargon.

**Structure:** Walk through one full season, narratively, with diagrams.

**Beats:**

1. **Roots** — what you bring in. Your offer, your proof, your brand voice, your constraints. Bristlecone reads them once and remembers.
2. **Soil** — the channels and audiences you're working with. Email, LinkedIn, direct outreach, content. You pick what's appropriate; we don't pretend to know your industry.
3. **Plan a season** — the system proposes a Goal. You commit, revise, or push back. From the goal it proposes Strategies. Same gate. From each strategy it proposes Tactics. Same gate.
4. **Drop a cone** — when a tactic is ready to execute, the system shows you the artifact (email draft, post, ad creative) for final review. Nothing ships without your commit.
5. **Read the rings** — outcomes flow back into your history. Year over year, you can see what worked, what didn't, and why.
6. **Heartwood** — the governance substrate underneath. Every decision the system made is traceable. Every output has its inputs on record.

Use the existing **commit / revise / jurisdiction** vocabulary explicitly here — this is the page where the operator learns the language.

End with a small honest section:
> **What it can't do (yet)**
> - It doesn't run paid ads autonomously. You'll wire those manually.
> - It doesn't replace a strategist for category-defining decisions.
> - It doesn't write your offer for you. You bring that.

## `/for-whom`

**Purpose:** Self-selection. Help the right person feel found, and the wrong person feel respectfully redirected.

**Structure:** Two columns side-by-side, full height.

**Left column — "If you are…":**
- A solo operator or small team running a business you intend to keep for decades.
- A trades business, professional service, craft maker, or long-tail SaaS.
- Someone who has been burned by aggressive marketing tools and growth-hackers.
- Comfortable approving a draft email before it sends.
- Skeptical of "AI does it all" and prefer to keep your hand on the wheel.
- Patient. Or willing to become patient.

**Right column — "If you are…":**
- A funded startup with hockey-stick KPIs.
- Looking for fully autonomous marketing automation.
- In a hurry to triple revenue this quarter.
- Allergic to reading drafts before they ship.

Closing line, centered:
> *Bristlecone is built for the first column. If you're in the second, we wish you well — and we mean it.*

## `/pricing`

**Purpose:** Transparent. No tricks. No "Contact us" on the entry tier.

**Structure:** Three tiers, paper-card treatment. Annual default toggle to monthly (no "save 20%!" badge nag — just the math).

Tiers (placeholder; finalize before launch):

- **Sapling** — for one operator running one brand. $X/mo. Single Grove. Up to N seasons per year.
- **Grove** — for a small team or a portfolio of brands. $Y/mo. Multiple Groves. Methuselah Mode included.
- **Old Growth** — for established small businesses with multi-year planning. $Z/mo. Everything in Grove plus a quarterly working session with us.

Below the tiers:
- A short FAQ block (5–7 questions, plain answers): refunds, data ownership, cancellation, do you train models on my data (no), can I export my history (yes, always), what happens if Bristlecone goes away (your data is yours, here's the export format).
- A small italic note: *"We are not VC-funded. Pricing exists to keep the lights on and the team small. If pricing is a real barrier for your business, write to us."*

## `/changelog`

**Purpose:** Proof of life. Real changes, dated, honestly described.

**Structure:** Reverse-chronological list. Each entry: date, one-line title, 2–3 sentences of detail. No marketing speak.

Tone example:
> **2026-04-28 — Honest-resolution guards**
> Added contract checks so the system can't silently fall back to a worse channel. If you ask for LinkedIn outreach and that transport isn't wired up, you'll see it in the gate output instead of getting a mailto link by mistake. Boring. Important.

## `/journal`

**Purpose:** Long-form writing. Slower than a blog. ~1 piece per month, not per week.

**Structure:** Index page lists pieces with title, date, reading time, one-line tease. Detail page is just type — generous margins, no sidebar, no "related posts" carousel, no share buttons that ping trackers.

First three pieces to commission/draft (placeholders):
1. *"Why we don't have a growth chart on our homepage."*
2. *"The bristlecone pine, the metaphor, and the math."*
3. *"What 'operator-in-the-loop' actually costs you, and what it buys."*

## `/about`

**Purpose:** Who we are. Short, honest.

**Structure:** A few paragraphs. Names of founders/team. What we did before. Why we're building this. Where we are based.

Don't pad with mission/vision/values headers. The philosophy page is the values page.

## `/waitlist`

**Purpose:** Single conversion. No distractions.

**Structure:** Centered card on parchment. Short headline, two-paragraph explainer, email field, submit. Post-submit: a real thank-you page that says *what happens next* (e.g., "We'll write to you when there's a seat. We don't send marketing emails in the meantime.").

No nav on this page (or minimal). No footer except legal links.

## `/manifesto`

**Purpose:** The full belief stack, expanded. Linked from footer, not nav. For people who really want to know what they're buying into.

**Structure:** ~2,500 words. Subheaders. Personal voice. Signed by the founder(s).

This is the page someone shares when they want to convince a partner or co-founder. Make it shareable on its own merit.
