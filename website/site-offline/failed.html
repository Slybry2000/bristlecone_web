What failed.

When we miss a delivery commitment, it gets listed here with the date, the operator we owed it to, what went wrong, and what they were owed back.

This page exists before it needs to. That is on purpose. A commitment with no public failure mechanism is not a commitment.

How this page works.

Every Bristlecone delivery commitment names a deadline, a deliverable, and what we owe back if we miss. When we miss, here is what we publish — and, just as importantly, what we don’t.

  1. The miss is ours. Each entry describes what Bristlecone failed to do. It does not describe the operator’s behavior, their list, their brand, or their business model. The cause sits with us.
  2. Operators stay private by default. We identify them only by industry and rough geography — for example, “an independent architecture practice in Northern California.” An operator may choose to be named, but only with a signed release, and the release includes the right to retract that naming at any time, for any reason.
  3. The release does not waive statutory rights. Nothing here overrides GDPR or similar law. If an operator asks us to erase or correct an entry, we do — even after the fact. We have retired the “never removed” line. It read well and it was wrong.
  4. Forty-eight hour cooling-off review. No entry is published the day of the miss. Every draft sits for two days and passes two pairs of eyes — one of them from outside the team that owned the miss.
  5. What an entry contains. The date of the missed deadline. The industry and rough geography of the operator (or their name, with release). What went wrong on our side, in plain language. What they received back — refund, extension, replacement deliverable, or some combination.
  6. Not retroactive. Entries are added within seven days of the miss, unless legal review needs longer. We don’t backfill.

Why we built this before we needed it.

A delivery commitment without a public failure mechanism is a marketing line. We’re a new tool asking skeptical operators to trust a 90-day timeline. The honest version of that ask includes the page where we’ll show our work when we miss, before we’ve had the chance to miss.

Public accountability has limits, and we’d rather name them than pretend otherwise. Privacy law, defamation, and breach of confidence all sit on the other side of the line from candor. The guards above — the operator’s privacy by default, the right to erasure, the cooling-off review, the discipline of describing only our own conduct — are what make this commitment honorable instead of reckless.

If this page stays empty, good. If it doesn’t, you’ll know.
